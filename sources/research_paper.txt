Research Summary: Machine Learning Approaches to Epileptic Seizure Detection Using EEG Signals

Background
Epilepsy affects an estimated 50 million people worldwide, and timely seizure
detection from EEG recordings remains a critical challenge in clinical neurology.
Manual review of EEG data by specialists is time-consuming and prone to human error,
motivating automated detection approaches.

Methodology
This study evaluated a hybrid approach combining deep learning and ensemble methods
on EEG signal data. A convolutional and recurrent neural network architecture was
used to extract temporal and spectral features from raw EEG channels, while a
gradient-boosted ensemble model (XGBoost) was trained on handcrafted statistical
features including signal variance, spectral entropy, and peak frequency.

Dataset
The dataset consisted of EEG recordings sampled at 256Hz from multiple electrode
channels, labeled by clinicians into seizure and non-seizure segments. Data was
split into training, validation, and test sets with careful attention to avoiding
patient-level data leakage between splits.

Results
The combined neural network and ensemble approach achieved 93.4% classification
accuracy on the held-out test set, with a sensitivity of 91.2% and specificity of
94.8%. The neural network alone achieved 89.1% accuracy, while the ensemble model
alone achieved 87.6%, indicating the hybrid approach outperformed either method
individually.

Discussion
False negatives in seizure detection carry higher clinical risk than false
positives, since a missed seizure delays intervention. The study's sensitivity of
91.2% suggests the model still misses roughly 1 in 11 seizure events, indicating
room for improvement before clinical deployment. Feature importance analysis showed
spectral entropy was the single most predictive handcrafted feature.

Limitations and Future Work
The dataset was drawn from a limited number of patients, raising questions about
generalization to broader populations. Future work should incorporate multi-center
data and explore real-time inference constraints for wearable EEG devices, which
have stricter compute and power budgets than offline analysis.
